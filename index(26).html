The Bad-Habit Tracker

Awareness is the first move. You can't change what you won't see.

1. Your Goal

What's the one thing you want to achieve? Keep it specific enough that you'll know when you get there.

2. What's My Why

According to Darren Hardy, your goal won't survive contact with reality unless you have a deep why. And you can't build a real why without first knowing what you actually value. Work through the four exercises below — write freely, no one's grading you. Then look for the themes that repeat across your answers. Those are your core values.

From values to why: Your values are what matters. Your why is what you'll do, become, or protect because those things matter. Look at your top 3–5 values and ask: who am I trying to be, and what am I trying to honor or protect, by chasing this goal? Write that as one sentence you can come back to when things get hard.

Exercise 1 — Peak Moments

Think of 3–5 moments when you felt most alive, proud, or fulfilled. Not the biggest moments — sometimes the quietest. For each, ask: what was being honored or expressed? Connection? Creativity? Mastery? Freedom? Service? The themes that repeat are pointing at your values.

Exercise 2 — The Frustration Test

What consistently pisses you off or makes you righteously angry? Values often show up as their inverse — if injustice enrages you, justice is probably a core value. If micromanagement makes you crazy, autonomy matters to you.

Exercise 3 — The Eulogy Exercise

Imagine your funeral in 40 years. What do you want people to say about who you were and how you lived? Not what you accomplished — who you were. The qualities you hope they name are your values.

Exercise 4 — The Non-Negotiables Question

What would you not compromise on, even for a lot of money or status? Those lines you won't cross are values in disguise.

3. How to Track (Hardy's Principles)

Awareness is the whole point. Hardy asks: "What am I doing that I didn't consciously choose to do, yet continue to do every day? Which of those behaviors support me? Which pull me down?" The reason tracking works isn't the data — it's that the act of recording forces consciousness onto an unconscious behavior. Just knowing you'll have to write it down makes you do less of the bad thing in real time.

Start with one habit. Pick the single bad habit most blocking your goal and track only that one. Trying to track everything at once is how people quit by week two. Once you have momentum, expand.

Track everything related to it for one week. Hardy recommends spending the first week writing down every instance of the behavior — every drink, every scroll session, every cigarette, every dollar spent. You'll be shocked at where your time, money, or attention is actually going.

Then keep tracking for two more weeks. Week one is shock. Weeks two and three turn that shock into awareness. You start noticing the small things you're doing right or wrong, you make small course corrections, and the results begin to compound.

Course correction is normal. If you've derailed or feel like you're heading the wrong direction, tracking will reveal it. You're not trying to be perfect — you're trying to see clearly so you can adjust.

Be patient. Old habits have deep roots. The longer you've had a habit, the longer the unwinding takes. Forget the "21 days" myth.

Watch for the ripple effect. One slip tends to lead to another ("I already blew it, might as well..."). One win leads to another. Recognize the spiral when it starts so you can break it early.

4. My Bad Habits

This is the core of the app. Each habit you add holds everything related to it: your triggers (the Big 4), your clean-house actions, your swap, and a running log of every time it happens.

Identify your triggers — the Big 4. For every bad habit, map the who, what, where, and when that prompts it. Who are you with? What are you doing right before? Where are you? When in the day does it happen? You can't disrupt a pattern you haven't mapped.

Clean house. Eliminate the environmental cues. Drink too much? Get the alcohol out of the house. Watch porn? Install blockers on your devices. Doomscroll? Move the apps off the home screen, or delete them. Make the bad habit harder to access than your willpower at 11 p.m.

Swap it. Replace the habit with a healthier behavior triggered by the same cue — or drop-kick it entirely. The swap works best after you've cleaned house, because the trigger has less force.

Reminder: Hardy says start with one. Add the others to the list if you want, but pick one to actively track this week.
